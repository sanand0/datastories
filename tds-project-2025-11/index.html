The Token Question

There's an uncomfortable confound I can't fully resolve. Several students reported running out of API tokens as they progressed through the exercise. The assessment was designed to be solved by a single chained programâ€”which meant token consumption compounded with each question.

The gate was testing ability to decompose novel problems, debug iteratively, and combine LLM capabilities in ways the questions didn't explicitly suggest. Students who could do this passed quickly. Students who couldn't burned through tokens without progress.

What This Means

The gated structure of this assessment created a cleaner test of LLM problem-solving ability than a traditional exam would have. By forcing students through a skill filter early, it separated those who could think with AI tools from those who could only use them for straightforward tasks.

The finding that matters: 37% of students demonstrated genuinely higher LLM problem-solving ability, as evidenced by their success on questions that required complex reasoning, iteration, and tool orchestration. Another 19% tried hard but couldn't cross the threshold. And 25% never seriously engaged.

For educators designing AI curricula, the implication is clear. Exposure to LLM tools isn't enough. Students need deliberate practice on the meta-skill of working with AIâ€”decomposing problems, debugging prompts, recognizing when an approach isn't working, and pivoting to alternatives.

The gate was harsh. But it measured something real.
