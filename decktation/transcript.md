Consultant: Hey, thanks for making the time. I know you’re slammed. I’ll keep this tight. To start, when you say “client reporting,” what exactly are we talking about—quarterly reports for which products?

Ops Manager: Yep, quarterly client reports for our equity funds. So, commingled equity vehicles, a couple of separate accounts that follow the same reporting pack. Same rhythm every quarter: compile, review, distribute.

Consultant: Got it. Walk me through the core workflow end-to-end. Like, “data comes from X, then Y happens, then PDF goes out.”

Ops Manager: Sure. Step one: we request the quarter-end data from our third-party fund administrator—Vistara. They send back what they call the “reporting bundle.” Step two: we reconcile that against our internal numbers, basically to make sure holdings, cash, fees, performance, all line up. Step three: we populate our report templates—these are formatting-heavy PDF templates—with the validated numbers. Step four: IR reviews commentary and sign-off. Step five: we send to clients and upload to the portal.

Consultant: When you say “reporting bundle,” what format is it coming in?

Ops Manager: That’s… the pain point. It’s raw and unstructured. Some parts are Excel extracts, some are PDFs, sometimes it’s a ZIP with multiple versions, and the naming conventions aren’t consistent. You’ll see “Final_FINAL2” kind of stuff. And then we have to manually reconcile it.

Consultant: So the bottleneck is the intake and reconciliation from Vistara, before it even hits your templates?

Ops Manager: Exactly. If Vistara gave us clean, structured tables with stable field names, we’d be in a different world. Instead, we spend days interpreting what they meant, matching it to our internal ledger, and then copy-pasting into the templates.

Consultant: Let’s quantify it. What’s the current effort and headcount?

Ops Manager: It’s three FTEs dedicated for about two full weeks every quarter. That’s basically their life during reporting season—data collation, reconciliation, and formatting. And it’s not just “busy.” It’s brittle. One person out sick and we feel it immediately.

Consultant: Three FTEs, two full weeks, every quarter—just for data collation and formatting. Not including review cycles?

Ops Manager: Correct. Review cycles add time, but the heavy lift is getting the numbers into the right shape and into the PDF templates. The templates are… I mean, they’re a nightmare. Very brand-driven. Lots of locked formatting, tables that break if you breathe on them.

Consultant: Where do handoffs happen? I’m listening for “this team does X, then throws over the wall.”

Ops Manager: Vistara to FundOps is the big one. Then FundOps to IR for commentary and final checks. Sales also gets pulled in because they want certain client-specific angles, but that’s more ad hoc. And Compliance sometimes pops in late with “did you include the right disclosures.”

Consultant: Quick clarification: is the commentary written by FundOps or IR?

Ops Manager: Mostly IR. But we feed them a draft, honestly. We provide the standard paragraphs, the “market backdrop,” that sort of thing, because they’re also stretched.

Consultant: And quality-wise—what’s working, what isn’t?

Ops Manager: Look, our primary goal is getting the reports out the door on time. That’s the KPI that matters. But yes—IR and Sales complain a lot that the commentary is repetitive and lacks analytical depth. They’ll say, “This reads like last quarter with the dates changed.” And… they’re not wrong. We don’t have time to do deep analysis when we’re fighting formatting issues.

Consultant: Understood. Where do errors creep in—if they do?

Ops Manager: Manual copy-paste is the obvious one. Also version control. Vistara sends updates, then we’re not always sure which number set made it into which template version. We’ve got checklists, but it’s still messy.

Consultant: If I waved a wand, what would you change first?

Ops Manager: Standardize the intake from Vistara. Give me structured data, predictable fields, and a reconciliation report that ties out cleanly. Second, reduce dependency on these PDF templates—at least automate population. Because right now, three people spending two weeks per quarter on collation and formatting is just… not a good use of talent.

Consultant: Last one: what does “success” look like a year from now?

Ops Manager: Reports out on time without heroics. Maybe one FTE doing oversight instead of three people in the trenches. And commentary that actually says something new—so IR and Sales stop rolling their eyes.
